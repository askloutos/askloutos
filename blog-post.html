Why the Dutch outlived their creditors

KENT LAWRENCE / 12 JUL 2026 / 9 MIN READ

In 1671, a Dutch city treasurer named Johan de Witt sat down to solve a problem that had nothing to do with tulips or trade routes: how much should a city charge for a life annuity?

The actuarial accident

What followed was one of the first rigorous attempts to price a human life — not out of scientific curiosity, but because the city of Holland was broke and needed to borrow money from its own citizens.

Tontines and their morbid math

Related instruments spread across Europe. Tontines paid survivors more as their co-investors died, turning mortality into a market signal decades before insurers formalized the practice.
